## Bank — Marlowe & Finch (lines 44–71)

### Open the first envelope (L51)
On the desk, an envelope with no return address. Inside, a single hand-painted card — **The Wheel of Fortune**, the numeral **X** in the corner — and a note in looping script.

> No fortune favors the unannounced. What is locked will open.

No date. No demand. Only the promise. The card is yours now; it joins *The Cards*.

### Unscrew the brass air vent (L57)
The screws turn too easily — their slots are bright where a fresh tool bit into the brass. Someone has had this grate off recently, and put it back with care. The shaft beyond runs down toward the vault corridor, just wide enough for a thin man.

### Pull the maintenance ledger (L61)
Years of signatures for work on the locks and the vault door. One name recurs through the early entries, the contractor who overhauled the mechanism six years ago: **FELIX MARSH, locksmith**. The later pages are someone else's hand. He has not signed since.

### Open the visitors' book (L66)
Most entries are dull — couriers, clerks, the auditor. One oddity: the tenor **Enrico Belloni** signed in three times in a single week this spring, each visit only minutes long. A famous voice has no business in a bank vault three times in seven days.

## Madame Vesna's Shop (lines 72–121)

### Dialogue (L76–108)

**Show her a painted card**
She turns the Wheel over in her hands and her mouth tightens. *"These are mine. My deck, my hand — except they are not my hand at all. Someone has copied them, stroke for stroke."* She sets it down. *"Hold a copy against the original and a trained eye finds where the copyist strayed. Borrow my deck. Look for yourself — I will not do your seeing for you."*

**Borrow her original deck**
She wraps the deck in black silk and presses it into your hands. *"Mine are honest. Lay them beside the forgeries on your own table. Where they differ, the copyist meant something."* Vesna's true cards now sit on the table beside yours.

**Ask about Felix Marsh**
*"Felix. Yes — years a client, and a reader himself, better than most."* She almost smiles. *"But he never sat under his own name. In the circle he read as a card. **The Fool** — the wanderer who carries nothing, fears nothing. He liked that it is numbered **zero**. 'The only honest number,' he'd say, 'the one you count toward when everything else is spent.'"*

**Ask about his peculiar reading**
*"Ah. That."* She cuts an imaginary deck and turns the halves over. *"Felix cut the deck upside down. Every card he read **reversed** — always. 'Fortune only tells me the truth standing on its head,' he used to say. It drove the others mad. He never once read a card the right way up."*

**Ask about the order of the cards**
She lays the three painted cards in the order they came. *"Ten. Then nine. Then eight."* Her finger taps each numeral. *"These were not dealt, detective. They were **counted**. One each morning, each a step lower than the last. A deck does not do that on its own. A man does — a man marking off the days to something."*

**Ask about the altered lantern**
She holds the forged Hermit beside her own. *"My hermit carries a star in his lamp — the light of one distant truth. The copyist gave his lamp a **full moon** instead."* She taps the painted disc. *"A man does not change a thing like that by accident. He wanted the moon in the picture. Why, I leave to you."*

### Hotspots (L109–120)

**Study the lunar almanac on the shelf**
An unmarked almanac, open to **June**. The month's phases are printed plainly: last quarter on the **6th**, **full moon on the 14th**, lunar perigee on the 17th. Nothing is circled. Nothing is noted. It simply tells the truth about the sky.

**Glance at the crystal ball**
You lean toward the glass. Vesna does not. *"Decoration, detective. The cards do the work."* Your own face, upside down and small. Nothing else.

## Scotland Yard (lines 122–139)

### Read Felix Marsh's case file (L128)
A thin file, mostly ruin. Felix Marsh, locksmith, lost his savings when a fund manager defrauded his clients. The fund's reclusive backer, the multimillionaire **Nicholas Neo**, repaid every investor from his own pocket. Felix **refused the money** — too proud to take charity from a rich man — and chose a grudge instead. Six years of silence. Then a painted card in a bank's morning post.

### Pull the bank's insurance schedule (L134)
Marlowe & Finch's five most valuable boxes, declared for cover. Gold. Bearer bonds. A collection of stamps. The tenor **Belloni**'s box, lightly insured. And, alone in its phrasing, **Box 35** — holder *N. Neo* — contents declared in a single word: **"priceless."** No figure. No itemization. Just the word.

## Felix's Workshop (lines 140–166)

### Examine the practice vault door (L146)
A full vault door, bolted to the workshop wall and drilled a hundred times over — the same door the bank uses, or near enough. The floor beneath is bright with metal filings. He has done this entry, in the dark of his head, more times than you can count.

### Unpin the card above the door (L150)
Pinned dead-centre above the practice door, where a man would see it every working hour: **The Fool**, numbered **0** — a figure stepping cheerfully off a cliff-edge into open air. The pin has rusted into the plaster. It has hung here a long time. It is his, and it joins *The Cards*.

### Photograph the wall calendar (L155)
A cheap wall calendar, June showing. One date is ringed in pencil, gone over and over until the lead tore the paper and dented the plaster behind: **the 11th**. No note beside it. No reason given. Just the ring, pressed hard enough to mean something — or to look as though it does.

### Search the wall of orphaned keys (L161)
Dozens of keys on numbered hooks, every tag aged to the same brown — except one, newer than the rest, the ink barely faded: **"The Crown & Anchor, cellar — NOT FINISHED."** A job he never came back to close.

## Royal Opera House (lines 167–189)

### Ask the stage doorkeeper (L173)
The old doorkeeper knows Belloni's habits to the minute. *"Before every opening night he sends a boy to fetch a little parcel from the bank, and the morning after he sends it straight back. Never opens it where anyone can see. Superstition, with singers — you learn not to ask."*

### Ask the tenor's dresser (L178)
The dresser lowers her voice. *"It's his mother's brooch. She sang here, before him. He wears it pinned inside his waistcoat, against the heart, where not a soul in the house will ever see it. He'd sooner lose his voice than go on without it."* A parcel from a vault, three times in a week. A son who misses his mother. Nothing here is missing, and nothing is strange.

### Search the prop room and its locks (L184)
Among the prop locks Felix once serviced for the theatre, slipped behind a flat of painted scenery, a single card: the **Seven of Swords** — a figure carrying off five blades and leaving two standing behind him. Left here, not mailed. It joins *The Cards*, though it keeps no numeral's place in the morning count.

## The Crown & Anchor (lines 190–207)

### Ask the landlady about Felix (L196)
*"Felix? Course I knew Felix. Rebuilding my cellar door all winter, good work too — then one week in spring he just stopped coming."* She nods at the wall. *"Left his tab unpaid and his own tankard still on the hook. Not like him. Something took him off and never gave him back."*

### Examine the unfinished cellar door (L201)
Half a lock set into the old door and stopped there — like a sentence broken off mid-word. The work is immaculate as far as it goes. His tools are still in their canvas roll on the floor, laid out in order, waiting for a hand that stopped coming in spring. Honest work, abandoned. It says everything about the man and points nowhere.
